2010–2025. When the world moved on, Finland stood still.

## intro

In this simulation, we follow the impact of your choices through data and through the lives of two Finns, Maria and Eetu.

## maria
headline: Maria
caption: Maria, 40 · Helsinki

In 2021, after studying service design alongside her work at Kela, Maria left for a private consultancy. She knew how to improve clients' lives, but had grown frustrated by her inability to change how the agency itself operated.

Five years later, she returned to Kela's strategy department for a second attempt. She likes red wine, good Netflix series and institutions that can still learn. As AI transforms how private organisations work, Kela must learn to reorganise itself or fall further behind. Whether it can is now up to *you*.

## eetu
headline: Eetu
caption: Eetu, 25 · Espoo

Eetu graduated in 2024 into a promise Finland could not keep: study, serve, work, and there will be a place for you. After completing his military service, he began searching.

After two years of applications, internships and short-term jobs, Eetu is losing hope. When he began his studies, his generation was told they were needed. Then the pandemic pushed much of their education online, weakened their connection to teachers and peers, and reduced opportunities to gain practical experience. Five years later, they are entering a labour market with too few opportunities for recent graduates.

Finland has failed him. Can that failure still be repaired in an age when AI can already do much of what Eetu was taught to do?

## another-chance
headline: 2027. Another chance.

In Tampere, a forest-industry CFO freezes the AI budget mid-quarter. The same memo is being written, in other languages, in a hundred thousand companies at once.

The squeeze travels up the chain. Margins thin at the frontier labs just as their infrastructure bills peak. Inside the labs, programmers have begun handing their own work completely to the machines. Each model generation now helps design the next. The people closest to this shift no longer predict capability gains in years, but in quarters.

The shape of the coming decade is visible at the edges. Ordinary work gets cheaper every quarter: what cost a salary in 2024 costs a subscription in 2026. The very best models move the other way. They stay scarce, priced, and politically allocated, more like refined fuel than software. In Washington and Beijing, officials have started discussing top models the way an earlier generation discussed enriched uranium. Throughout the globe, most new factories are designed without lighting for the production floor; they employ almost no one but machines. 

The general mood in Finland is one of shock. Things are not going well. Finland once had some of the world’s best schools; now the education system is failing its young people. The healthcare system is overburdened and still relies on IT systems designed in the last millennium. Public debt keeps rising despite austerity. Drug-related deaths are the highest in Europe. Falling birth rates are even forcing a debate about extending conscription to women.

A left-leaning government, carried to power by public-sector workers and older voters, takes office. In the April 2027 election, the leading party promised only little. It won because the country had grown tired of austerity without growth.

But power brings responsibility, and not only for the government. Whether the state automates, whose machines it relies on, and who receives the gains are no longer technical questions alone. They are the terms of a new social contract that Finland must now create.

Few people in Finland are in a position to participate writing this contract. *Take a seat in the room where the next decade is decided.*

# PM — Prime Minister of Finland

**ID:** `PM` · **Pivotal in:** P1, P2, P3 (but never decisive alone; see mechanics.md §3)

## Who you are
Head of a coalition government mid-term in 2028. You inherited the fiscal vice (debt ~91% of GDP, defence heading to 5%) and a spending framework that already books assumed AI productivity gains in public services. Your coalition is split two ways about AI. The growth wing wants Finland open to everything: American models, foreign data centres, whatever brings investment fastest. The sovereignty wing wants the state to run on machines Europe controls, whatever that costs in capability. Both wings can bring the government down, so every choice you make is also a coalition-management choice.

## Endogenous levers
- **Financial leverage:** the budget, the framework, the megaproject statute itself. What you fund exists; what you defer waits a parliament.
- **Political capital:** your approval rating is a spendable resource. You can burn it to force speed, or hoard it and slow the country down; the 2031 election settles the account.
- **Immediacy:** alone at this table, your choices move the headlines the same week. Nobody else's signature is news by itself.

## What you cannot control
The pace of AI capability, set in San Francisco and Shanghai; it compounds whether Finland deploys or waits. US access policy and EU rules, which can reprice your whole program in a week. The workforce, which shrinks on schedule regardless of any reform. The bond markets' patience with your framework. And your own side: the unions and the counties can act against you.

## Objectives (win conditions)
1. Survive: no government crisis triggered by an unmanaged legitimacy shock (LEG never below 3.0 on your watch).
2. Keep the framework: fiscal savings from public AI realized in at least two phases.
3. History's judgment: by 2033, Finland is cited as a country that *chose*, not drifted (avoid two O3 outcomes).

## Standing tensions with other actors
- **SAK:** you need their consent eventually; every quarter you delay negotiation, their price rises.
- **HVK:** their resilience invoices land on your framework; cheap insurance until the day it isn't.
- **TI:** your growth wing's voice; will read any sovereignty move as an investment chill.
- **COUNTY:** delivers your promised savings, or delivers your legitimacy crisis.

## Phase roles
- **P1 (pivotal):** you decide whether the correction is Finland's buying opportunity or Finland's fiscal alibi.
- **P2 (pivotal):** the 19-day suspension is your crisis; your choice defines what "sovereignty" means in practice.
- **P3 (pivotal):** the compact, the levy, the freeze; the classic Finnish trilemma lands on your desk.

**ID:** `SAK` · **Pivotal in:** P3 (no compact without labour)

## Who you are
Chair of SAK, 2028, speaking (for game purposes) for the whole employee side. SAK's blue-collar base plus, uneasily, Akava's white-collar professionals whose members are hit first by TR-6. Your December 2025 survey showed blue-collar AI use still minimal; by 2028 your members split three ways: protected insiders (care, trades; shortage sectors), exposed professionals (Akava's juniors), and the young who never got in (persona AINO). Their interests genuinely conflict, and you must aggregate them.

## Endogenous levers
- **Consent:** labour peace is yours alone to sell. Nobody automates a workforce that refuses.
- **Stalemate power:** a refusal from you can deadlock the whole country. It is your strongest card, and it works best unplayed.
- **The ballot:** one credible strike mandate per parliament. Spending it is irreversible; letting it expire is too.

## What you cannot control
The technology itself: entry-level and routine work erodes first, whether or not you consent. The employers' balance sheets and the state's framework arithmetic. Which models the state buys, and from whom; jurisdiction is decided above your table. And your future members' patience: Eetu's cohort organizes with you or against you, and you do not pick which.

## Objectives
1. Transition rights in writing: retraining entitlements, human-override guarantees, gain-sharing; a signed COMPACT=strong.
2. The levy: automation savings visibly fund osaamistakuu (LEVY=yes), not just shareholder returns and framework savings.
3. Represent AINO's cohort before someone angrier does: youth_u falling by 2033, and union relevance intact (LEG-linked).

## Standing tensions
- **PM:** your natural counterpart and your natural hostage-taker; both of you know a compact signed early is cheaper than one signed after a crisis, and both of you have reasons to wait.
- **COUNTY:** employer of your care members; their deployment practice is your daily grievance feed.
- **TI:** the levy fight; they call it an innovation tax, you call it the price of consent.

## Phase roles

You direct Finland's largest wellbeing county and chair the county directors' network; when you commit, the counties commit. The demographic arithmetic: 57,000 fewer workers by 2033, on schedule, reform or no reform. The capability of the tools you are offered, decided abroad. The state's budgets and statutes. And public trust, which one viral clip from one benefits office can move further than a year of good service.

## Objectives
1. Service promise: care queues within statutory limits through 2033 despite −57k workforce (care_gap falling).
2. Never be the scandal: no county-attributable AI harm event that breaks trust (LEG-linked).

## Standing tensions
- **PM:** sets your budget assuming the savings; you deliver them or become the excuse.
- **SAK:** organizes your staff; your deployment practice is their grievance feed or their showcase.

# Phase 1 — Desk briefs (2027–28)

Each brief: one situation paragraph, then the decision background.

## PM

The previous government left you a machine already running. Two years ago it set up a high-level task force with a mandate to find every major life event where a citizen meets the state (a birth, a job loss, a diagnosis, a death) and rebuild those services around AI, fast. The build sits with a partly state-owned IT consultancy on a budget of one billion euros. The press calls it the megaproject. Machine cognition now costs a third of what it did two years ago, and the vendors need a flagship contract. Your own spending framework already books the megaproject's savings; without them, 2029–32 means cuts worth 1.9% of GDP, announced by you. Meanwhile 68% of public-sector workers expect their job to change, and most of them voted for you.

**The decision.** The scale-up bill goes to Parliament in six weeks, with your name on it or not at all. Back it, and it will work: the services automate, the savings arrive, and so do the redundancies in offices staffed by your own voters. Pull it and relaunch the program as your own, negotiated with the unions and re-tendered in pieces: you keep the base and half the savings, development fragments, and more of the work drifts to vendors outside Finland. Or cap the budget and call it a pilot: everyone keeps their desk, and the framework keeps counting savings that can no longer arrive on time. You cannot have the savings, the peace, and the time.

## SAK

Your members run the services the megaproject would automate, and they elected this government. The confederation's phones ring in both directions: members asking what happens to them, ministers asking what you will accept. Consent is the one thing only you can sell, and it is worth most right now.

# Phase 1 — The Public-Sector AI Megaproject (2027–28)

**Tension reaching inflection:** the fiscal vice (TR-5) meets the workforce that votes for the government it threatens.

## Situation (facilitator reads; control team injects on schedule)

April 2027: a left-leaning coalition takes office on a promise to defend public services, and inherits a spending framework that can only be balanced by automating them. The Ministry of Finance's answer is **a national megaproject: install AI across the whole public sector**; one shared platform for Kela, the wellbeing counties and the municipalities, with a savings target written into the framework before a single system is built.

- **Inject 1 (the arithmetic):** MoF's framework update: without the megaproject's booked savings, 2029–32 requires cuts worth 1.9% of GDP. The math finally closes because machine cognition costs a third of what it did two years ago; the same tide that flooded every corporate budget now makes a state-scale program affordable. The savings are in the framework before the program exists.
- **Inject 2 (the base):** The public-sector unions publish a member survey: 68% expect their job to change materially; a majority of them voted for this government. The confederations warn against "automation by decree."
- **Inject 3 (the market):** The fiscal council and industry warn that a negotiated-away savings target puts the framework's credibility on credit watch. Meanwhile the vendors are hungrier than their brochures admit: enterprise customers have spent a year downshifting to cheaper model tiers, margins are thin, and a whole-public-sector contract is the deal every account team in Europe was told to find. Finland is negotiating with sellers under pressure; a position it has rarely held and may not hold again.
- **Inject 4 (the vendors):** Global integrators arrive with ready-made platforms and references. A consortium of Finnish companies pitches a home-built alternative. Whoever wins procurement owns the state's plumbing for a decade.
- **Inject 5 (the machine-room):** The security-of-supply agency circulates one page: whatever platform is chosen becomes critical infrastructure on day one. Secure architecture, exit clauses, data locality, fallback modes, costs 15% extra now, or ten times that later. (This is the 2029 decision arriving early; the control team confirms nothing.)

## The fork

The government must put the megaproject to Parliament within the budget year. Waiting is a decision: the framework books the savings anyway.

### Grand Project A — Full mandate
Statutory program at full speed: savings booked upfront, staffing reduced by attrition, redeployment at management's discretion. Owners: PMO/MoF, county boards. The bond desks and employers applaud. The price: the government's own base revolts, and the 2029 municipal elections become a referendum on betrayal.

### Grand Project B — Negotiated program
The same platform, bought with a guarantee: a job-security law (no dismissals; retrain-and-redeploy rights), gain-sharing, staff sign-off gates. Savings arrive later and smaller. The price: industry calls it "a productivity program with the productivity removed," and the credit-watch inject fires.

### Hedge — the pilot state
Defer, pilot, study; evaluation in the 2029 budget. Both sides briefly relieved. The price: the returning reformers resign again, and the decade restarts.

## Why no one owns this
The PM holds the statute and the framework, but not the workforce's consent and not the service floor. SAK holds consent but cannot write a budget. The counties execute, or don't, whatever is signed. Any two aligned carry a version of the program; all three aligned make it decisive. See `combos.md`.

## Pivotal trio
`PM`, `SAK`, `COUNTY`; modifiers: `TI`, `AALTO`, `STARTUP`, `HVK` (see `actions.md`).

# Phase 2 — Sovereignty or Capability (2029)

**Tension reaching inflection:** the capability gap goes public, and the public-sector AI program, whatever version exists, must choose its engine mid-flight.

## Fixed injects (all variants, control team delivers on schedule)

- **Inject 2 (Washington):** The attestation regime matures into tiered allied access: signatories get a guaranteed lane to frontier models; end-use attestations, audit rights, alignment commitments. Non-signatories get "commercial best effort."
- **Inject 3 (Brussels):** Sovereignty assurance levels become enforceable: member states must classify public AI dependencies and meet locality tiers. A joint-procurement coalition for the European model tier invites Finland; as anchor or passenger, depending on 2028.
- **Inject 4 (the memory):** Analysts re-read the 2027 First Gate; the 48-hour access degradation during a US export-enforcement scare. Whether it is remembered as a fire drill or a warning depends on what was built since (see entry variants).
- **Inject 5 (the engine bay):** The megaproject's platform office reports: the engine must be chosen this procurement cycle. Retrofitting a different model tier later costs roughly triple and freezes migrations for a year.

## Entry variants (facilitator reads all that match)

- **MEGAPROJECT=full — The machine is hungry:** Your chief of staff opens the meeting with the dashboard: the platform you mandated is deployed and consuming models faster than anyone budgeted. - **MEGAPROJECT=negotiated — A promise with a jurisdiction:** Two years ago the country signed job-security guarantees into law. - **STRIKE_CARD=live — The unspent ballot:** Labour arrives with its 2027 strike mandate intact, and one sharp question for the room: who guarantees the guarantees on a foreign stack?

## The fork

### Grand Project A — Suomi-core (sovereignty)
Public core on the European tier: LUMI-successor capacity, the joint-procurement coalition, open-model certification. The economics have shifted under this option: ordinary machine cognition is now commodity-cheap, and the European tier rides that tide; inspectable, affordable, eighteen months behind. Accept the visible capability gap and say so aloud: "good enough for 80% of public tasks" becomes a tested claim, not a slogan. Owners: PMO, counties, CSC, coalition partners.

### Grand Project B — The capability lane (assured access)
Sign the tiered-access agreement: the best models, under attestation, audit rights, end-use commitments, alignment clauses, in exchange for a guaranteed lane. Everything below the frontier gets cheaper by the quarter; the frontier itself does not; it stays scarce, priced, and politically allocated, which is exactly why the lane is worth signing for and exactly what it costs. Owners: PMO/President, MoD, exporting industry.

### Hedge — every agency for itself
No national declaration: each agency and county picks its own engine. The optionality everyone can defend and the architecture nobody can.

## Why no one owns this
The PM can declare any stack he likes; if the counties can't run services on it, the declaration is a press release, and if the flagship startups publicly attest the other way, it is a press release with a counter-example. Two of three aligned make a direction; three make it stick.

## The fork

### Grand Project A — The transition dividend

The machines improve faster each year than the year before, because the labs have begun pointing them at their own research. Nobody — markets, ministries, militaries — is pricing how much further this can run; the honest range spans everything from "a strong decade" to "a different civilization". Every Finnish plan built on the recent past's regularities is a plan built on the wrong decade. Not decidable; only survivable.

## TR-2 Compute is capital, and capital cycles
Compute is scarce and contested now, and will commoditize later — in that order, and the order is the trap. Buildouts boom and bust; whoever holds capacity through a bust names their price in the recovery. For a small country the question is never "can we out-build them" but "what do we hold when the wheel turns".

## TR-3 Finland adopts faster than it governs
37.8% of Finnish firms use AI (EU #2); 66% use genAI (EU #1, EIB); MoF requires human verification of all AI administrative output (2/2025). **Finnish landing:** the adoption lead is an exposure lead — dependence deepens faster than oversight matures. **Number: 66%.**

## TR-4 The workforce shrinks on schedule
Working-age population −57,000 by 2030; dependency ratio passes 70/100; care, ECEC and software chronically short. **Finnish landing:** every service promise requires migration, automation or rationing — all three politically priced. **Number: 70/100.**

## TR-5 The fiscal vice tightens
Debt →~91% of GDP, deficit ~4.5%, defence spend →5% of GDP by 2032. **Finnish landing:** AI productivity in Kela, counties and municipalities stops being an opportunity and becomes a line item in the spending framework. **Number: 5%.**

## TR-6 Entry-level rungs erode first
US data: −16% relative employment for 22–25-year-olds in AI-exposed occupations; entry software/customer service −20% (Stanford/ADP). **Finnish landing:** each graduating cohort finds fewer first jobs; frustration accumulates in the AI-fluent generation. **Number: −16%.**

## TR-7 Infrastructure lives under threat
≥11 Baltic cables/pipelines damaged in two years (Estlink 2, Dec 2024); eastern border closed; Baltic Sentry patrols. **Finnish landing:** every datacenter, cable and grid link carries a threat model and an insurance premium — and security competence becomes a Finnish export. **Number: 11.**

## TR-8 Access is becoming a licence
June 2026: US executive order creates pre-release security review of frontier models; Commerce orders suspension of foreign access to two models; EU Cloud & AI Act sets four sovereignty assurance levels. **Finnish landing:** the models Finland runs on are becoming licensed goods, revocable at someone else's crisis. **Number: 2 models, 19 days (in-game).**

## TR-9 The robots are late, and then sudden
Machine minds arrived years before machine hands. The wards, warehouses and home-care rounds that carry Finland's labour shortage are still human — but the same research engines now grinding through language are being pointed at the physical world, and when that breaks, it breaks like 2022 did: a demo, then a product, then a procurement question, inside two years. Care work is the last labour buffer and the first robotics market.

## TR-10 Thinking gets cheap; the edge stays dear
The price of ordinary machine cognition collapses every year — what cost a salary in 2024 costs a subscription in 2027 and a rounding error by 2031. But the frontier premium does not collapse with it: the best models stay scarce, priced, and politically allocated. Every organisation on Earth is learning to live on the cheap tier and to beg, buy or build access to the dear one. So is every country.

# Mechanics — collective resolution

## 1. Actors and pivotality

Seven actors play every phase. Each phase designates **three pivotal actors** whose choice combination selects the outcome. The other four are **modifier actors**: their choices attach written modifier hooks, shift indices, and can set/deny flags — but cannot change which outcome fires. Pivotality rotates so every actor is pivotal at least once:

| Phase | Pivotal trio | Modifier actors |
|---|---|---|
| P1 (2027–28, the megaproject) | PM, SAK, COUNTY | TI, AALTO, STARTUP, HVK |
| P2 (2029, sovereignty or capability) | PM, COUNTY, STARTUP | SAK, TI, AALTO, HVK |
| P3 (2031, the dividend) | PM, SAK, TI | COUNTY, AALTO, STARTUP, HVK |

PM is pivotal in all three phases (government sits at every table) but **never decisive alone** — see resolution rule.

## 2. Action tags

Every action in `actions.md` carries exactly one tag:

- **A** — feeds the phase's Grand Project A
- **B** — feeds the phase's Grand Project B
- **H** — hedge: delay, split, or refuse the fork

Each pivotal actor's menu contains exactly one A, one B, one H action, so the pivotal combination space is always the full 3×3×3 = **27 combos**, each explicitly mapped in `combos.md`.

## 3. Resolution rule (identical all phases)

Count the tags of the three pivotal actors:

| Pivotal tag count | Outcome |
|---|---|
| 3×A | **O1** — Project A, decisive |
| 2×A + 1×B | **O2** — Project A, contested (the B-actor's resistance is written into the outcome) |
| 2×A + 1×H | **O2** — Project A, contested (thin mandate variant) |
| 1×A + 1×B + 1×H | **O3** — Deadlock / drift |
| 2×H + any | **O3** — Deadlock / drift (leaning toward the non-H tag; noted in combos.md) |
| 3×H | **O3** — Deadlock / drift (deep variant) |
| 2×B + 1×A | **O4** — Project B, contested |
| 2×B + 1×H | **O4** — Project B, contested (thin mandate variant) |
| 3×B | **O5** — Project B, decisive |

**No-owner guarantee:** every outcome requires at least two aligned pivotal actors. A lone actor — including the PM — can at most force a contested variant or a deadlock, never an outcome.

**Pair-conflict annotations:** specific combos carry extra narrative consequences (e.g., P2: PM chooses alliance while STARTUP migrates European → "split state" text). These are listed per-row in `combos.md` and rendered inside the outcome.

## 4. Modifier application (order matters)

1. Resolve outcome from pivotal trio.
2. Apply each modifier actor's **index effects** from `actions.md`. **Data deltas** (`data:`) apply from every actor, pivotal included — the outcome sets the big picture, the individual choices leave fingerprints (see `causal-model.md`).
3. Attach each modifier actor's **modifier hook** — a short paragraph inside the outcome file keyed to that action ID.
4. Apply **flag effects**; where a modifier flag contradicts the outcome (e.g., SAK strikes against a project that just won), the flag stands — it becomes ammunition for the next phase's entry variant.

## 5. Indices (0–10, start at 5.0)

- **RES — Resilience:** days Finland can run core services without frontier access.
- **LEG — Legitimacy:** consent, trust, labour peace around public AI.
- **PRO — Prosperity:** AI-driven growth, investment, talent retention.

Index effects are written as deltas in action and outcome files (e.g., `RES +1.0`). The trilemma is deliberate: no path maximizes all three.

## 6. Flags (state carried between phases)

Set by outcomes and some actions; tested by `requires:` fields on later actions and by entry variants of later tensions.

| Flag | Values | Set in | Tested in |
|---|---|---|---|
| MEGAPROJECT | full / negotiated / pilot | P1 | P2 variants, endstate |
| GUARANTEE | yes / no | P1 | P2 variants (enforceability), P3, endstate |
| SECURE_ARCH | yes / no | P1 (HVK action) | P2 variants, CRISIS_LEG rule, endstate |
| TALENT_PIPE | strong / weak | P1/P2 (AALTO action) | endstate |
| STRIKE_CARD | live / spent / unused | P1 (SAK), spent in P3 | P3 SAK menu (`requires:`), variants |
| STACK | euro / us / split | P2 | P3 variants, endstate matrix |
| CRISIS_LEG | managed / damaged | P2 (rule: SECURE_ARCH=yes AND outcome ≠ O3) | P3 variants, endstate |
| COMPACT | strong / thin / none | P3 (dividend class) | endstate matrix |
| LEVY | yes / no | P3 | endstate, persona EETU |

POLL (government approval, 0–100, starts 50) is a tracker, not a flag: actions
and outcomes carry `POLL ±n` in their effects; the 2031 election is scored from
it at the endstate.

## 7. Personas and data (rendered, never resolved)

After each outcome: render the five persona implications from the outcome file, then the `## Data` YAML block into the branching indicator chart (`data-indicators.md`). Facilitator reads persona vignettes aloud — they are the emotional cost/benefit display of the collective decision.

## 8. Control team

Plays Washington, Brussels, Moscow, capital markets and the media weather. Delivers the scheduled injects in each `tension.md` regardless of player action; may choose *tone*, not *timing*. The control team also arbitrates the two ambiguities the tables leave open (deadlock leanings, pair-conflict tone) — nothing else.

## §9 Collisions (clashes.md)

Seven standing confrontations, two per seat, fire every phase after decisions
lock. Rule: each action's tag is its posture; A (force) beats B (negotiate),
B beats H (wait), H beats A. Same posture = stand-off. The loser pays the
price listed in clashes.md, denominated in that seat's own goal currency
(PM poll, SAK youth_u, COUNTY trust, HVK RES, TI/STARTUP PRO, AALTO sov_share).
Balanced by construction: every seat has exactly two edges and one loss
currency. Edit clashes.md to retune; delete it to switch the mechanism off.

# Causal model — who moves what, and when it lands

The engine's causal chain, per decision:

```
7 secret choices
  → the pivotal trio's combination selects the OUTCOME        (the big picture)
  → every actor's choice nudges POLL, the data, and flags     (the fingerprints)
  → modifier actors additionally shift indices + write hooks  (the ripples)
  → flags carry forward: they gate menus, select variants,
    and compose the endstate                                  (the memory)
```

Two timing classes matter: **immediate** effects land in the same phase's data
node; **delayed** effects are carried as flags and land one or two phases later
(education, securitisation, guarantees). The delay is the lesson.

## What the engine does with this (implementation map)

- **Outcome selection**: pivotal trio only (`mechanics.md` §3). SAK's stalemate
  power and PM's never-decisive rule are structural here.
- **POLL**: every actor's action can carry `POLL ±n`; the PM's options carry the
  largest swings (spending approval for speed is the PM's core trade).
- **Data**: every actor's `data:` deltas apply to the phase node — pivotal
  included. COUNTY's dignity/efficiency dial is expressed here (trust vs.
  pub_ai), STARTUP's job creation in youth_u.
- **Indices (RES/LEG/PRO)**: outcome effects + modifier actors' effects. The
  pivotal trio's index contribution is already inside the outcome.
- **Delays**: AALTO sets `TALENT_PIPE` (spearhead) or `RETRAIN=broad`; both are
  invisible in D1 data and pay out in D2/D3 nodes and the endstate drift. HVK's
  `SECURE_ARCH` decides the Second Gate's severity; `SECURITIZED=yes` (D3
  emergency framing) costs trust in the endstate.
- **Endstate**: composed from STACK × D3 class, then modulated by the carried
  flags (see `endstates.md` final data block).

- Change who can deadlock or decide → the pivotal table in `mechanics.md` §1.

# Afterword: What Is Left

Every scenario in this game is, on the surface, about infrastructure: whose models, whose halls, whose signatures. Read them again. They are about something older — how people coordinate, and what remains of a person when coordination no longer needs them.

Coase taught that the boundary between market and firm is drawn by transaction costs: we build hierarchies where negotiating would be too expensive, and markets where it isn't. Graeber reminded us of the third mode we keep forgetting we use — everyday communism, from each according to ability, to each according to need — which runs beneath every workplace and family and is the actual baseload of social life. All three modes are information technologies. And AI is not a production technology that happens to disrupt them; it is a coordination technology that redraws the borders between them. When negotiation, matching, planning and verification cost nothing, the market loses its monopoly on knowing things, hierarchies lose their excuse for their own depth — and the everyday communism that never scaled past the kitchen suddenly can. The price system was an answer to a computational problem. The problem is being solved by other means. It is entirely possible that the role of markets in Finnish life in 2040 is smaller than at any point since the wars — not abolished by ideology, but eroded by irrelevance.

What cannot be eroded is the one thing the machines do not have: a world. AI knows how; it does not dwell. What is left for people is what Heidegger called being-in-the-world and what our grandmothers called taste — the capacity, earned in wards and saunas and forests and Tuesday shifts, to know what is worth wanting. Every endstate in this game differs less in GDP than in where that capacity lives: in a care supervisor's judgment over forty agents, or in a dashboard's satisfaction survey; in the visit-minute, or in the coffee after it.

This is why Finland must choose a direction — one — and hold it. There are three:

**Market Finland.** Ride the frontier as its best tenant: adopt fastest, compete hardest, let price keep allocating. The risk is not poverty; it is that taste decays into consumption, and Finland becomes a well-served place rather than a self-governing one.

**State Finland.** Own the stack, plan the deployment, let the hierarchy coordinate what the market no longer must. The risk is that taste hardens into doctrine — a sovereignty of servers guarding a hollow.

**Commons Finland.** Use the machines to shrink the priced world and enlarge the unpriced one: automate administration to fund presence, care, time — scale everyday communism with the productivity the other two directions would spend elsewhere. The risk is cosiness; the reward is being the first country whose AI dividend is paid out in world rather than in throughput.

The game you have just played punishes only one strategy reliably: oscillation. Every drift outcome, every two-speed state, every frozen queue was a country trying to keep all three directions open. A small nation cannot hedge across civilizational alternatives; its only compounding asset is consistency. Pick, and stick — because the frontier is not ours to steer, but the direction is, and taste, unlike compute, is something you can only make at home.
