# Phase 1 — Desk briefs (2027–28)

Each brief: one situation paragraph, then the decision background.

## PM

The previous government left you a machine already running. Two years ago it set up a high-level task force with a mandate to find every major life event where a citizen meets the state (a birth, a job loss, a diagnosis, a death) and rebuild those services around AI, fast. The build sits with a partly state-owned IT consultancy on a budget of one billion euros. The press calls it the megaproject. Machine cognition now costs a third of what it did two years ago, and the vendors need a flagship contract. Your own spending framework already books the megaproject's savings; without them, 2029–32 means cuts worth 1.9% of GDP, announced by you. Meanwhile 68% of public-sector workers expect their job to change, and most of them voted for you.

**The decision.** The scale-up bill goes to Parliament in six weeks, with your name on it or not at all. Back it, and it will work: the services automate, the savings arrive, and so do the redundancies in offices staffed by your own voters. Pull it and relaunch the program as your own, negotiated with the unions and re-tendered in pieces: you keep the base and half the savings, development fragments, and more of the work drifts to vendors outside Finland. Or cap the budget and call it a pilot: everyone keeps their desk, and the framework keeps counting savings that can no longer arrive on time. You cannot have the savings, the peace, and the time.

## SAK

Your members run the services the megaproject would automate, and they elected this government. The confederation's phones ring in both directions: members asking what happens to them, ministers asking what you will accept. Consent is the one thing only you can sell, and it is worth most right now.

**The decision.** Your board sets the line on Thursday. Trade consent for a board seat and gain-sharing, and you shape the program from inside but own its failures. Demand job security in law, and you protect the members at the price of a slower, smaller program. Run a strike ballot, and your threat becomes real but the government becomes an opponent. Each choice spends a different currency, and none of them refunds.

## COUNTY

Your county loses care workers every month while the queues grow; the sector is short 57,000 people by 2033. Automation is your only staffing plan that adds capacity. The ministry wants the counties' implementation commitment in writing before the bill, and your answer becomes every county's answer.

**The decision.** Efficiency first means committing to the state's schedule: the queues shrink fastest, and nobody asks the person in them how it felt. Dignity first means consent gates and staff sign-off: trust holds, the wards keep their people, and the queues shrink slower. Waiting for proof is prudent and costs a year of backlog. You choose which promise you break.

## TI

You are asked on live television how industry feels about the largest public investment of the decade. Your answer becomes the association's position by morning. Your members have spent a year putting machine work on a budget line; they know what it is worth and what delay costs.

**The decision.** Back the full mandate and you speed the program but tie industry's name to a fight with labour. Back the negotiated version with a public audit clause and you get a slower program you can hold to account. Warn the markets instead, and you keep your hands clean while the government pays in basis points. Pick the failure you can defend to your members.

## AALTO

The ministry's letter asks what the university will retool for the program. Your budget board meets before the bill is voted: one faculty budget, committed for four years, on your signature. Whatever you fund now reaches the labour market around 2030. US labs are recruiting your best students with tools you cannot license.

**The decision.** Spearhead programs train the few hundred engineers who could build and audit the platform; the displaced wait. Broad retraining bridges ten thousand displaced clerks into new roles; the platform gets staffed by someone else. Protecting the core funds neither. One budget. You cannot educate both futures.

## STARTUP

The procurement pre-brief lands in your inbox at 9:04. Two investors ask before lunch what you will do. Your own inference bill eats your runway, every vendor's margins are thinner than they look, and this is the largest software procurement in Finnish history.

**The decision.** Bid as a domestic consortium and the state's plumbing stays in the country, with your name on the risk. Subcontract under a global integrator and the revenue is faster on someone else's platform. Pass, and you stay clean and locked out for a decade. Plumbing contracts do not reopen.

## HVK

Whatever platform is chosen becomes critical infrastructure on day one. Procurement law gives you one window to write security requirements into the tender: before publication. After it, every fix is a renegotiation with a vendor who has already won.

**The decision.** Demand exit clauses, data locality and fallback modes now, and the program costs 15% more and survives its first crisis. Accept certification instead, and the tender is cheaper and the protection is paper. Stand aside, and the gap gets a file. Nobody thanks you in 2028. Whether anyone thanks you in 2029 is decided this week.
