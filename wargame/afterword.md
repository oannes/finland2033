# Afterword: What Is Left

Every scenario in this game is, on the surface, about infrastructure: whose models, whose halls, whose signatures. Read them again. They are about something older — how people coordinate, and what remains of a person when coordination no longer needs them.

Coase taught that the boundary between market and firm is drawn by transaction costs: we build hierarchies where negotiating would be too expensive, and markets where it isn't. Graeber reminded us of the third mode we keep forgetting we use — everyday communism, from each according to ability, to each according to need — which runs beneath every workplace and family and is the actual baseload of social life. All three modes are information technologies. And AI is not a production technology that happens to disrupt them; it is a coordination technology that redraws the borders between them. When negotiation, matching, planning and verification cost nothing, the market loses its monopoly on knowing things, hierarchies lose their excuse for their own depth — and the everyday communism that never scaled past the kitchen suddenly can. The price system was an answer to a computational problem. The problem is being solved by other means. It is entirely possible that the role of markets in Finnish life in 2040 is smaller than at any point since the wars — not abolished by ideology, but eroded by irrelevance.

What cannot be eroded is the one thing the machines do not have: a world. AI knows how; it does not dwell. What is left for people is what Heidegger called being-in-the-world and what our grandmothers called taste — the capacity, earned in wards and saunas and forests and Tuesday shifts, to know what is worth wanting. Every endstate in this game differs less in GDP than in where that capacity lives: in a care supervisor's judgment over forty agents, or in a dashboard's satisfaction survey; in the visit-minute, or in the coffee after it.

This is why Finland must choose a direction — one — and hold it. There are three:

**Market Finland.** Ride the frontier as its best tenant: adopt fastest, compete hardest, let price keep allocating. The risk is not poverty; it is that taste decays into consumption, and Finland becomes a well-served place rather than a self-governing one.

**State Finland.** Own the stack, plan the deployment, let the hierarchy coordinate what the market no longer must. The risk is that taste hardens into doctrine — a sovereignty of servers guarding a hollow.

**Commons Finland.** Use the machines to shrink the priced world and enlarge the unpriced one: automate administration to fund presence, care, time — scale everyday communism with the productivity the other two directions would spend elsewhere. The risk is cosiness; the reward is being the first country whose AI dividend is paid out in world rather than in throughput.

The game you have just played punishes only one strategy reliably: oscillation. Every drift outcome, every two-speed state, every frozen queue was a country trying to keep all three directions open. A small nation cannot hedge across civilizational alternatives; its only compounding asset is consistency. Pick, and stick — because the frontier is not ours to steer, but the direction is, and taste, unlike compute, is something you can only make at home.
